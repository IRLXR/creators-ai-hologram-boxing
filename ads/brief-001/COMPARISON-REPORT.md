## Which version creates more mystery?

**Version B wins.**

Removing narration lets the viewer lean into visuals, bass hits, and negative space. The staged text drops (“THE FUTURE OF LIVE ENTERTAINMENT…”, then silence, then “IT’S HERE.”) read like a teaser—not an explanation. Version A tells the story out loud, which reduces ambiguity and curiosity.

---

## Which version feels more premium?

**Version B edges ahead; Version A is close behind.**

Both share the same approved cinematography, color grade, and soundtrack. Version B’s sparse copy and trailer-style pacing feel more luxury-launch (Apple/Nike energy). Version A still feels premium because Roman’s hype delivery and the ducking mix were already tuned for social—but spoken explainer lines (“you walk into the arena, gear up…”) slightly reduce the “exclusive event” aura.

---

## Which version feels more like a Hollywood trailer?

**Version B.**

Trailers often withhold explanation and use typography + score to build tension. Version B matches that grammar: title card energy → spectacle → punch-line text → CTA. Version A behaves more like a finished spot with a voice-over bed—strong, but closer to broadcast promo than opening trailer.

---

## Which version is likely to stop scrolling more effectively?

**Version A for cold audiences; Version B for retargeting and brand-aware viewers.**

- **Version A** hooks faster: the VO delivers the premise within the first 2 seconds (“Picture this—”). On TikTok/Reels, many viewers watch without sound initially, but paid placement often catches sound-on viewers—Roman’s energy is a pattern interrupt.
- **Version B** relies on motion + text for the first beat. Silent scrollers still get the 0–2s line, but mid-roll comprehension is lower without VO. For users who already saw the tent, headsets, or Wave, Version B’s mystery can outperform because it feels like “the next chapter.”

**Practical split:** use A for broad prospecting; use B for warm audiences and cinematic brand posts.
